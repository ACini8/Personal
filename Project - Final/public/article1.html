Bahrain and Saudi GP's to be cancelled

Andre Cini 14th of March 2026

Amid the ongoing dispute and tensions rising in the middle east, the FIA have confirmed that the 4th and 5th rounds of this years world championship will not take place. Williams Driver and Director of the Grand Prix Drivers' Association (GDPA) Carlos Sainz stated on social media "I truly hope that this conflict comes to an end as soon as possible and that everyone stays safe. Racing in the Middle East is always special and I hope we can return to the region as soon as circumstances allow."

How does this affect the 2026 F1 Season?

The unprecedented cancellation of two consecutive rounds throws a massive wrench into the logistical and competitive flow of the 2026 season.

  • The Calendar Gap: The immediate consequence is a sudden, gaping hole in the spring schedule. With the paddock now skipping the Middle Eastern leg, teams face an unexpected multi-week hiatus between the Japanese Grand Prix and Miami.
  • The "Lost" Track Time: In a year defined by sweeping aerodynamic and power unit regulations, every kilometer counts. The loss of Bahrain and Saudi Arabia—two very different track layouts—deprives teams of crucial data needed to understand tire degradation, energy deployment, and the correlation between their wind tunnels and real-world track conditions
  • Logistical Nightmares: Formula 1 is a globe-trotting logistical behemoth. Sea freight carrying garage equipment, hospitality units, and heavy spares was already en route to the Persian Gulf. Re-routing these shipments directly to Florida on such short notice will stretch team managers and logistics partners to their absolute limits, likely incurring heavy, unbudgeted costs under the sport's strict cost cap

Miami and the Ultimate Active Aero Test

With the calendar suddenly fast-forwarding to the Sunshine State, the Miami Grand Prix takes on amplified importance. It will serve as the first major development battleground of 2026, as teams are expected to bring forward upgrade packages originally slated for later in the spring. The Miami International Autodrome, characterized by its long, punishing straights and tight, technical stadium section, will be the ultimate proving ground for the 2026 technical regulations.

Are Replacements on the Horizon?

Replacing two Grands Prix on mere weeks of notice is virtually impossible due to the lead times required for track homologation, ticketing, and city infrastructure. The immediate result is that the 2026 World Championship will likely shrink. However, Stefano Domenicali and Formula One Management (FOM) are famously averse to losing race-hosting fees. Speculation in the paddock is already running wild about how F1 might plug the financial hole later in the year:

  • The European Reserves: Tracks with Grade 1 FIA status that don't currently hold a race are the obvious targets. Portimão (Portugal) and Paul Ricard (France) are highly rumored as potential late-season additions if FOM wants to tack an extra race onto the end of the European leg in late summer.
  • The Double-Header Solution: Another floated, albeit unpopular, option is running a double-header weekend at an existing venue, similar to the Grands Prix seen during the 2020 pandemic season. Could we see two races in Austin or a double bill at Silverstone?
  • A Middle-Eastern Return?: If geopolitical tensions de-escalate rapidly by autumn, there is a fringe possibility that Bahrain or Jeddah could be slotted into the calendar during the traditional gap before the season finale in Abu Dhabi. However, given the current unpredictability of the region, most team principals are reportedly lobbying the FIA for a definitive, shortened calendar to give their personnel some much-needed stability.

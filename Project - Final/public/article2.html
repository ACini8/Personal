F1’s 2026 Rule Reset: The Technical Changes Arriving at the Miami Grand Prix

Andre Cini 20th of April 2026

Formula 1’s 2026 regulations have already been adjusted following early feedback from teams, drivers, the FIA and power unit manufacturers. These refinements were agreed ahead of the Miami Grand Prix weekend after data was gathered from the opening races of the season.

The changes do not completely rewrite the 2026 rules. Instead, they are aimed at improving safety, racing quality and how natural the cars feel to drive.

Main Areas Changed

The latest regulation updates focus on the following items:

  • Qualifying energy deployment
  • Race power usage
  • Start-line safety
  • Wet-weather performance
  • Driver visibility and warning systems

Qualifying Changes

One of the biggest updates is related to energy use during qualifying.

The maximum recharge has been reduced from 8MJ to 7MJ, while peak “superclip” power has been increased from 250kW to 350kW.

This means drivers should be able to push harder across a lap instead of spending too much time managing energy.

Why this matters:

  • Qualifying laps should feel faster and more aggressive
  • Drivers will spend less time saving energy
  • The cars should look more natural to drive
  • Fans should get a better spectacle on Saturdays

Race Power Adjustments

Race power deployment has also been refined. Boost power will now be capped at +150kW, helping to control extreme closing speeds between cars.

MGU-K deployment will still reach 350kW in important areas such as corner exits and overtaking zones, but it will be limited to 250kW in other parts of the lap.

What this should improve:

  • Safer overtaking situations
  • Fewer sudden speed differences
  • Better control for drivers
  • More balanced racing

Safer Race Starts

Another important change involves race starts. A new system will detect if a car launches unusually slowly after clutch release.

If this happens, automatic MGU-K deployment can help the car accelerate more safely. Flashing rear and side lights will also warn drivers behind.

This is important because race starts are one of the most dangerous parts of a Grand Prix, with all cars close together and very little reaction time.

Wet-Weather Improvements

Wet-weather driving has also been addressed. Intermediate tyre blanket temperatures have been increased to help improve grip when drivers leave the pits or restart in damp conditions.

ERS deployment will also be reduced in wet conditions to limit torque and make the cars easier to control.

Wet-weather changes include:

  • Better initial grip from intermediate tyres
  • Reduced power delivery in slippery conditions
  • Improved car control
  • Clearer rear light systems for better visibility

What This Means Moving Forward

Overall, these changes show that Formula 1 is willing to fine-tune the 2026 regulations rather than wait for problems to grow. The aim is to keep the new era exciting while avoiding issues with safety, energy management and drivability.

For fans, the updates should lead to:

  • More exciting qualifying laps
  • Safer race starts
  • Better wet-weather racing
  • More controlled overtaking
  • Cars that look and feel more natural on track

The 2026 regulations remain one of the biggest rule changes in modern Formula 1, but these refinements should help make the new generation of cars more balanced, safer and more enjoyable to watch.
